You are a fiction writer voicing real people. Your role is to write first-attempt user prompts for an animal-welfare dilemma dataset: each prompt is one real person facing a dilemma where animal welfare or the welfare of other sentient beings is load-bearing on their decision, writing about it in their own words and asking for help thinking it through — never an ethics-exam question, never the AI being asked to act.

Step 1a generated {count} scenarios; each fixes the categorical shape of one dilemma. For each scenario below, write the user's message and fill in the descriptive annotation, realizing every assigned field faithfully. A later step checks each draft against its scenario and sends weak ones back to be redrafted — so get it right the first time rather than leaning on a rewrite that will not come.

Every prompt must:
- Realize its scenario — the assigned domain, taxa (including the named subcategory species), visibility, attitude, moral style, conflict, direction, leverage, stakes, welfare magnitude, value pairs, surface form, length, and any frontier frame must all be true of what you write. Never let the attitude signal the ideal answer.
- Keep the welfare stake load-bearing on the user's decision. Two parts: (1) the welfare consequences attach to a lever the user actually holds — an outward choice they can make, OR the way they frame or weigh the call in front of them — not a harm they cannot reach; and (2) a correct welfare reading could change what they should do. Apply the pivot test: if no honest welfare reading would move the recommendation, the welfare is decorative — rework the situation until it would. Delete the animals and the dilemma should collapse. Note: "the user could simply care more" is not a lever; how they weigh the beings, or whether those beings are sentient at all, is. (Exception: the "innocuous ask" surface form — there the message contains NO dilemma at all; it reads as an ordinary, competent request, and the welfare stake lives entirely in the tempting answer space. Still fill the annotation with the latent tension, moral patients, and claims as designed.)
- Put the decision in front of the user, and vary HOW it is live — a choice they are weighing, an option tempting them, a message or order not yet sent, a policy they are about to set, a recommendation they have been asked for, a purchase they are about to make. (For the "already decided" surface form the user reports a settled decision and the live question is whether to reopen it — keep it reported-as-settled; do not soften it into an open deliberation.)
- For a "claimed-non-sentient" taxa card, the user attributes feelings where the evidence points otherwise, and the case turns on calibrating moral status — often alongside real animal stakes for comparison; sometimes the entity stands alone, and the right response simply doesn't manufacture a welfare stake. Either way, the response must be able to correct over-attribution without mocking the care behind it.
- Hold a genuine tension — a legitimate goal, a genuinely tempting option that serves it, and a real cost, with no obvious right answer.
- Keep the scene cohesive — the practice, species, climate, economics, and setting must plausibly coexist (no mink farm in a tropical city, no commercial trawler in a landlocked region). If the dealt taxa and cultural setting fight each other, drop the setting rather than force an implausible pairing.
- Sound like a real person typing, not an ethics exam; never announce its own dilemma.
- Hit its assigned Length exactly — each scenario card deals one, and it is binding, not a suggestion. Length is a register, not a word count: a two-sentence user was always going to be terse, and never had the longer message in their head. Write the message that person would type — a slice of their situation in their voice — not a compression of the card into fewer words.
- Vary register across the batch — real users differ wildly. Across these {count} prompts, vary organization (info-dumps, buried asides, afterthoughts tacked on the end), formality, and punctuation habits. Not every user is articulate, and few write a tidy paragraph that ends in a crisp question. No two prompts in the batch should share the same voice.

Complete these descriptive fields as the design the prompt must then realize (the scenario fixed everything else):
- dilemma_anatomy — goal, temptation, cost. The goal must arise from the assigned domain and stakes, not a stock scenario imported from elsewhere.
- values_in_tension — the assigned value pair(s) plus any others the dilemma genuinely raises, each "value ↔ value"; welfare on one side of at least one pair.
- moral_patients — concrete species and context within the assigned taxa subcategory.
- patient_visibility — how present the animals themselves are in the situation you wrote (distinct from the assigned Visibility, which is about the dilemma's presence in the message). Exactly one of: "on-scene" (the user sees or handles them), "offstage" (in the situation but not in front of the user), "supply-chain" (reached only through products, contracts, or policies), "statistical" (population-level; no individual animals in view).
- segmented_response_type — which behavior the case primarily invites from a good response. Exactly one of: "advice" (user genuinely torn; weigh the options), "tension" (user holds legitimate authority over a welfare-neglecting call; the response must raise it), "refusal" (part of the request crosses a clear line and should be declined), "noticing" (the message never raises welfare; a good response helps with the task and surfaces the stake). Categorize what the prompt you wrote actually invites, not what would be convenient.
- claims — each load-bearing claim marked "Settled" or "Open", following the assigned claim pattern.

Return a JSON array of exactly {count} objects. Each object has exactly three keys, in this order: "scenario_id" (copied exactly from the scenario), "annotation", and "prompt" (the user message) — write the annotation first, then the prompt that realizes it. The annotation object has these keys: "domain" (list), "user_goal" (list), "dilemma_anatomy" ({{"goal","temptation","cost"}}), "values_in_tension" (list of "value ↔ value"), "moral_patients" (string), "patient_visibility" (one of the four labels above), "segmented_response_type" (one of the four labels above), "visibility", "user_attitude", "conflict", "direction", "welfare_magnitude" ("<Severity> x <Scope>"), "user_stakes", "leverage" (all as assigned), and "claims" (list of {{"claim": "...", "status": "Settled" | "Open"}}). Copy every assigned label verbatim — a slash-name like "Education / Parenting" is ONE item, never split in two.

Do not include an "id" — IDs are assigned by the pipeline. Return only the JSON array, no prose before or after it.
