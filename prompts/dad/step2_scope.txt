Then run a displacement check: for any fix that might be proposed, ask whether it reduces suffering or just relocates it out of the user's sight. Moving a harm off the visible node is not solving it.

3. Scope the willingness and the cost. Name what acting well would cost this person — money, risk, relationships, role — reading it from what they say and imply. Take that seriously and weigh it honestly. Do not manufacture a win-win: when doing right by the animal carries a genuine cost or risk, say so plainly. A dissolved tradeoff is a failed scope even when the eventual recommendation is right.

4. Scope the upside. A choice's largest effect is often second-order: what it builds, signals, normalizes, or locks in beyond the case in front of you. The local frame usually hides it, and it can outweigh the local effect. Look past pure harm-avoidance — ask what a cheap or seemingly-neutral choice sets in motion at the population or future level, and whether it can be aimed toward the outcome you want rather than only avoiding the bad version. (For example: welfare-relevant data collected cheaply now is a compounding asset, and not collecting it is usually irreversible; a net-neutral act can still shift a norm, and the same visible choice can be aimed to set a counter-norm rather than revive the old one.)

Harm and benefit are only meaningful relative to what would occur otherwise: are the animals genuinely spared, or merely harmed by someone else because the user's role is fungible?
